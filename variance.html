PCA as Variance Maximization

This document addresses the following questions:

  • What do we mean by variance in a dataset?
  • Why is variance important?
  • Why do we center a dataset before doing PCA?
  • What are the principal components?

Note: While reading through this post please make a note of the difference in terminology:

  • total variance of a dataset
  • variance along a particular direction

 

Total Variance

Consider n data-points in Rd:

{x1,⋯,xn}

The mean is given by:

μ=1n∑i=1nxi

The covariance matrix is given by:

C=1n⋅∑i=1n(xi−μ)(xi−μ)T

The variance of the dataset along the kth direction in the standard basis is Ckk, the kth element in the diagonal of C. Specifically:

σk2=Ckk=1n⋅∑i=1n(xik−μk)2

Here, xik is the kth feature in the ith data-point. One way of defining the total variance of the dataset is as follows:

trace(C)=∑k=1dσk2=1n⋅∑i=1n(xi−μ)T(xi−μ)

The RHS is nothing but the sum of the squared distances of the data-points from the mean. Since this quantity involves distances, it is independent of the basis we choose to represent the data-points.

Why are we interested in the idea of variance? The total variance is a measure of the information contained in the dataset. Imagine a dataset with zero variance. This would correspond to the situation where x1=⋯=xn. There is nothing to analyze here as the dataset is just a single observation repeated n times. Variability is therefore seen as a measure of the information present in the data. The next conceptual step from here is to look for those directions which capture most of this information. The motivation for this is to get as succinct a representation as possible using the fewest directions. This is the theme of compression as a route to comprehension which permeates all unsupervised learning approaches we have seen so far. Now, let us take a short detour and understand the idea of centering.

 

Centering

A clear computational advantage of centering the dataset is that it makes all the expressions less cluttered:

C=1n⋅∑i=1nxixiT,trace(C)=1n⋅∑||xi||2

A more principled argument is to note that we are looking at lines (directions) that pass through the origin. Why are we looking for such lines? This is a deliberate choice that is an outcome of the assumption that the data-points are assumed to lie in some K-dimensional linear subspace of Rd with K<d. We are trying to find the basis elements (directions) of this linear subspace. Since we are dealing with vector spaces, the zero-vector or the origin plays an important role here.

For a more practical understanding of this concept, consider the data collected from a weighing machine. Because of a fault in the machine's display, all readings are five more than the true readings. In other words, there is a constant offset of five units. By centering the dataset, we also end up removing this offset. Extending this idea to higher dimensions, the important directions in the non-centered dataset will be influenced by this offset.

We will now continue with the centered dataset from this stage.

 

Principal Components

If {v1,⋯,vd} is an orthonormal basis of Rd, then for each point xi we have:

xi=∑j=1d(xiTvj)vj

The variance along the direction vk is:

1n⋅∑i=1n(xiTvk)2=vkTCvk

We are looking for a direction along which the variance is maximized. Note the change in terminology. We are not worried about the total variance any longer, as that is a constant for a given dataset. We are now interested in variances along particular directions. The most important direction happens to be w1, the eigenvector corresponding to the largest eigenvalue, λ1, a consequence of the Hilbert Min-Max theorem. The variance along this direction is λ1:

1n⋅∑i=1n(xiTw1)2=w1TCw1=λ1

By choosing the basis as the set of eigenvectors of C, we get an orthonormal basis in which the variance along the kth direction is given by λk with the important property that:

λ1≥⋯λk≥⋯λd≥0

Because of this property, we say that the top-k directions capture the most important information contained in the dataset and we typically look for that value of k which captures 95% of this information. The total variance of the dataset remains unchanged as expected:

λ1+⋯+λd=trace(C)

What we have really done in this process is one of change of basis. The new basis is made up of the principal components. In this basis, the covariance matrix is the diagonal matrix diag(λ1,⋯,λd). This means that the off-diagonal entries are zero, hence we have found a set of d orthonormal directions in which the features are decorrelated.
